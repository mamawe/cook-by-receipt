How to Keep Herbs Fresh: 3 Methods That Add Weeks of Life

📅 Updated July 2026⏱️ 5 min read✅ Fact-checked
Quick Answer

Treat soft herbs (parsley, cilantro) like flowers: trim stems, place in a glass of water, and cover with a plastic bag in the fridge. This keeps them fresh for 2–3 weeks. For hardy herbs (rosemary, thyme), wrap in a damp paper towel and store in a bag. For long-term storage, freeze chopped herbs in olive oil using ice cube trays — they'll last 6 months.

The Critical Distinction: Soft vs. Hardy Herbs

Not all herbs are stored the same way. The first step to keeping herbs fresh is knowing which category they fall into:

  • Soft herbs (tender stems, high water content): Parsley, cilantro, basil, dill, mint, chives, tarragon. These need moisture and will wilt quickly if neglected.
  • Hardy herbs (woody stems, low water content): Rosemary, thyme, sage, oregano, bay leaves. These prefer slightly drier conditions and last longer naturally.

Method 1: The Bouquet Method (Soft Herbs)

This is the gold standard for soft herbs like parsley, cilantro, and dill. It extends their life from 3 days to 2–3 weeks.

  1. Snip the stems. Cut about ½ inch off the bottom of the stems, just like you would with cut flowers. This opens up the stems to absorb water.
  2. Fill a glass with 1 inch of water. Don't use too much water — you don't want the leaves submerged.
  3. Place herbs in the glass, stem-side down, like a bouquet.
  4. Cover loosely with a plastic bag. A produce bag or zip-top bag works. This creates a humid micro-environment that prevents the leaves from drying out.
  5. Store in the fridge (for parsley, cilantro, dill, mint) or on the counter (for basil — basil turns black below 50°F).
  6. Change the water every 2 days. This prevents bacterial growth in the water.

Method 2: The Damp Towel Method (Hardy Herbs)

For rosemary, thyme, sage, and oregano, the bouquet method doesn't work as well — their woody stems don't absorb water efficiently. Instead:

  1. Lightly dampen a paper towel. Wring it out so it's barely moist, not wet.
  2. Wrap the herbs loosely in the damp towel.
  3. Place in a zip-top bag or airtight container. Don't seal completely — leave a small gap for airflow.
  4. Store in the crisper drawer. Hardy herbs will last 2–3 weeks this way.

Method 3: The Olive Oil Freeze (All Herbs, Long-Term)

When you have more herbs than you can use, freezing is the answer. The olive oil method preserves flavor better than water freezing and creates ready-to-use cooking cubes:

  1. Chop herbs finely and remove any tough stems.
  2. Fill ice cube trays about ¾ full with chopped herbs.
  3. Pour olive oil over the herbs until they're just covered. The oil prevents freezer burn and preserves the essential oils in the herbs.
  4. Freeze until solid (4–6 hours).
  5. Pop out the cubes and store in a freezer bag. Label with the herb type and date.
  6. Use directly in cooking — toss a cube into soups, sauces, or roasting pans. No need to thaw.

Frozen herb cubes last 6 months and are perfect for: basil in pasta sauce, rosemary with roasted potatoes, cilantro in curries, sage with brown butter.

Basil: The Special Case

Basil is the most temperamental herb. It turns black below 50°F, so never put it in the refrigerator. Instead:

  • Store on the counter in a glass of water (bouquet method), out of direct sunlight
  • Change the water daily
  • Use within 5–7 days for best flavor
  • If the stems are long, you can also treat basil like a plant: keep it in a pot with damp soil on a sunny windowsill

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you freeze herbs without oil?

Yes, you can freeze herbs in water instead of oil. However, oil preserves the fat-soluble flavor compounds better, so oil-frozen herbs taste more vibrant. Water-frozen herbs are fine for soups and stews where flavor gets diluted anyway.

Why does my basil turn black in the fridge?

Basil is a tropical plant sensitive to cold. Below 50°F (10°C), the cell walls rupture, causing oxidation that turns the leaves black. This is cold damage, not spoilage — but the texture and flavor are compromised. Always store basil at room temperature.
