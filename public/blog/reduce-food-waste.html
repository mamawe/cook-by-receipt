How to Cut Food Waste in Half

A practical zero-waste kitchen guide · Updated 2026-07-09

← cook by Receipt app · Zero-waste recipes

The average household throws out a shocking share of the food it buys — often a third of everything that comes through the door. The good news: you don't need compost bins or zero-waste certification to fix it. A handful of boring, repeatable habits will get you halfway there in a week.

The one idea behind all of this: cook and eat the things that expire first. Most waste isn't a lack of recipes — it's a lack of a plan that respects the calendar.

1. See what you actually have

You can't use what you can't see. A quick pantry-fridge scan — or a 60-second manual list — beats a mental note every time. The goal isn't perfection; it's a single place that tells you "you already own three open jars of mustard."

2. Plan around expiry, not around cravings

Build the week's meals from the items with the nearest expiry dates. That limp herbs-and-chicken scenario becomes "cook the chicken Tuesday, use the herbs in pesto Wednesday." When the plan follows the clock instead of the mood, waste drops on its own.

3. Treat scraps as an ingredient

Onion ends, carrot peels, herb stems, and celery leaves aren't trash — they're the start of a free stock. Keep a bag in the freezer, simmer it when full, and you've removed a whole category of waste while saving money on broth.

4. Shop from a list, not a feeling

Most impulse buys expire before they're used. A short list built from your plan keeps the cart honest. If it's not on the list and not a staple, leave it.

5. Log what you toss

One week of noting what you throw out reveals the pattern: always the spinach, never the rice. That single insight tells you exactly which habit to fix — and stops you buying the thing you keep wasting.
